## Who is Dr. Daniel G. Amen—and why is his work so significant?

Dr. Daniel Amen is a psychiatrist, brain researcher, and founder of the Amen Clinics in the USA. He became internationally known for doing something long considered unusual: he made the brain visible. Using functional imaging (especially SPECT scans), he examined hundreds of thousands of brains and recognized recurring patterns behind psychological symptoms.

His approach made him an authority in the USA—not only among professionals, but also for millions of affected individuals, parents, teachers, and therapists. He wrote numerous bestsellers, including *Change Your Brain, Change Your Life* and *Healing ADD*, and brought a central insight into public discourse:

**ADHD is not a single disorder, but a family of different brain profiles.**

This insight changes everything—diagnosis, self-image, treatment, and future perspectives.

## The Seven ADHD Types – A Map, Not a Judgment

What follows is not a medical diagnosis, but **orientation**. It serves self-understanding, not self-condemnation. Many people recognize themselves in several types—this is normal.

## 1. Classic ADHD

**Underactivation in the prefrontal cortex**

**Typical signs in everyday life:**
Difficulty starting or finishing tasks, impulsivity, inner restlessness, quick to lose focus.

**Central challenge:**
The areas for planning, focus, and impulse control are underactivated—especially with monotonous tasks.

**Helpful strategies:**
Clear daily structure, short work sessions, visual to-do lists, movement before mental work.

**Supportive nutrients & lifestyle:**
Protein-rich breakfast, omega-3 fatty acids, regular exercise, stable sleep rhythm.

## 2. Inattentive ADHD

**Low activity without outward hyperactivity**

**Typical signs:**
Mental fatigue, daydreaming, slow processing, little inner energy.

**Central challenge:**
Attention quickly fades without external stimulation.

**Helpful strategies:**
Morning light, activating environment, saying tasks out loud.

**Supportive nutrients:**
B vitamins, iron if deficient, omega-3, regular meals.

## 3. Overfocused ADHD

**Overactivity in the anterior cingulate cortex**

**Typical signs:**
Mental stuckness, perfectionism, difficulty with flexibility, rumination.

**Central challenge:**
Attention is hard to redirect.

**Helpful strategies:**
Mindfulness focused on letting go, conscious variety, new routines.

**Supportive nutrients:**
Magnesium, L-theanine, reduction of caffeine.

## 4. Temporal Lobe ADHD

**Unstable activity in the temporal lobes**

**Typical signs:**
Emotional irritability, memory lapses, mood swings.

**Central challenge:**
Emotional regulation and memory are inconsistent.

**Helpful strategies:**
Naming emotions, music and rhythm, fixed daily routines.

**Supportive nutrients:**
Omega-3, zinc, vitamin B6, stable blood sugar.

## 5. Limbic ADHD

**Underactivation in mood-regulating centers**

**Typical signs:**
Lack of drive, inner heaviness, negative self-talk.

**Central challenge:**
Attention problems are closely linked to low mood.

**Helpful strategies:**
Gratitude rituals, social connection, action before motivation.

**Supportive nutrients:**
Vitamin D, omega-3, folate, daylight.

## 6. Ring-of-Fire ADHD

**Global overactivation in the brain**

**Typical signs:**
Sensory overload, emotional intensity, racing thoughts.

**Central challenge:**
Too much activation prevents clear focus.

**Helpful strategies:**
Reducing stimuli, gentle movement, nervous system regulation.

**Supportive nutrients:**
Magnesium, anti-inflammatory diet, avoiding stimulants.

## 7. Anxious ADHD

**ADHD driven by anxiety**

**Typical signs:**
Constant tension, worry, loss of concentration under stress.

**Central challenge:**
Anxiety blocks attention.

**Helpful strategies:**
Breathing exercises with long exhalations, safety before performance.

**Supportive nutrients:**
Magnesium, L-theanine, reduced caffeine, sleep hygiene.

## The Central Insight: Individualization Instead of One-Size-Fits-All

The true value of Dr. Amen’s work lies not in the typology itself, but in the **dissolution of one-size-fits-all thinking**. What helps one brain may harm another. Therefore, the crucial question is not:

*What’s wrong with me?*
but:
**What does my brain need to come into balance?**

Many people with ADHD carry special gifts: creativity, empathy, intuition, originality. When these brains are properly supported, overwhelm transforms into potential.
